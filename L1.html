L.1 History of Jain Traditions and Sects

An interactive guide for JAB preparation (L.1)

An Eternal Religion

Indian culture has two main trends: Brahmanik (Vedic traditions) and Shramanik (ascetic traditions like Jainism and Buddhism). Jainism is an ancient, independent religion belonging to the Shramanik trend.

It's a common misconception that Bhagawän Mahävir founded Jainism. In reality, Jainism is considered an eternal religion that has always existed and will always exist. It is re-established in every time cycle by 24 Tirthankars. Archaeological evidence from the Indus Valley Civilization (3500-3000 BC) suggests the worship of the first Tirthankar, Rishabhadev, long before Mahävir's time.

The Time Cycles (Käl)

The universe goes through infinite time cycles. Each cycle is divided into two half-cycles:

  • Utsarpini: Ascending half-cycle, where happiness and virtues gradually increase.
  • Avasarpini: Descending half-cycle, where happiness and virtues gradually decrease. We are currently in the 5th Ärä (Dukham Ärä) of the Avasarpini cycle.

Key People in Jain History

From Tirthankars to modern reformers, many individuals have shaped Jain history.

Bridge builders who re-establish Jainism in each half-cycle.

  • Rishabhadev (1st): His worship is evidenced in the Indus Valley. India is named Bharat-varsha after his son, Bharat.
  • Neminäth (22nd): Cousin of Lord Krishna. Attained liberation on Mount Girnär.
  • Pärshvanäth (23rd): Lived 250 years before Mahävir and preached four vows (non-violence, truth, non-stealing, non-possession).
  • Mahävir (24th): Revived the Jain path and added the fifth vow of celibacy (Brahmacharya).
  • Ächärya Bhadrabähu: The Shrut-kevali who predicted a famine and led the migration to South India.
  • Ächärya Sthulibhadra: Led the monks who remained in the north during the famine and headed the first council at Pätliputra.
  • Keshi Ganadhar: Head of Bhagawän Pärshvanäth's followers who merged his community with Bhagawän Mahävir's.
  • Chandragupta Maurya: The emperor who abdicated his throne to become a Jain monk under Ächärya Bhadrabähu.
  • King Kharavela of Kalinga: A great royal patron of Jainism in the 2nd century BC.
  • Lonkäshah: A layman whose study of scriptures led to the formation of the non-idol worshipping Sthänakaväsi sect.
  • Virchand Gandhi: Represented Jainism at the 1893 World Parliament of Religions in Chicago and delivered 535 lectures in the USA and England.

Key Events & Timeline

Jain history is marked by several pivotal events that shaped its traditions and spread.

Around 300 BC, a 12-year famine in Magadh led to a critical division in the Jain Sangh. Ächärya Bhadrabähu led a large group of monks south to Shravanbelgola to survive, while Ächärya Sthulibhadra stayed in the north. The southern group strictly maintained the original practices, including nudity for monks. The northern group, facing harsh conditions, adopted the practice of wearing white cloth. When the southern group returned, they could not accept the changes, leading to the schism between the Digambar (sky-clad) and Shvetämbar (white-clad) traditions.

Traditions & Sects

While the core philosophy remains the same, Jainism is primarily divided into two main traditions, which are further divided into sub-sects based on ritualistic differences.

Key Differences: Digambar vs. Shvetämbar

Digambar

  • Monks are completely naked (sky-clad).
  • Believe women cannot attain liberation in their female form.
  • Believe the original Ägam scriptures are lost.
  • Believe Mahävir was not married.
  • Idols are plain, undecorated, with downcast eyes.

Shvetämbar

  • Monks and nuns wear simple white clothes.
  • Believe women can attain liberation.
  • Believe the 19th Tirthankar, Mallinäth, was a woman.
  • Accept the existing Ägam scriptures as authentic.
  • Believe Mahävir was married and had a daughter.
  • Idols are decorated and have wide-open eyes.

Shvetämbar Sub-Sects

Murtipujak

The original Shvetämbar tradition. They worship idols (murtis) of Tirthankars, offering flowers, fruits, and adorning them with jewels.

Sthänakaväsi

Arose in 1474 as a reform movement led by Lonkäshah. They do not worship idols and emphasize meditation. Their ascetics wear a Muhapatti (mouth-cloth) at all times.

Teräpanthi

Founded in 1760 by Swämi Bhikshu Mahäräj, who split from the Sthänakaväsi tradition. They are also non-idol worshippers and are known for their highly organized structure under a single Ächärya.

Digambar Sub-Sects

Bisapantha

They support the authority of Bhattäraks (heads of religious monasteries or Mathas). They use fresh fruits and flowers when worshipping idols.

Teräpantha

(Different from Shvetämbar Teräpanthi) Arose in 1627 as a revolt against the authority of Bhattäraks. They worship idols but do not use fresh fruits or flowers.

Täranapantha

Founded by Tarana-Swämi. They do not worship idols but worship sacred books instead.

Historical Strongholds of Jainism

Jainism has flourished in various parts of India throughout history, often with the support of powerful kings and dynasties.

East India (Magadh, Kalinga)

The birthplace of Jainism. Kings like Shrenik (Bimbisär) and dynasties like the Nandas and Mauryas (Chandragupta, Samprati) were great patrons. King Kharavela of Kalinga was another major royal patron.

South India (Karnataka)

Became a major center after Ächärya Bhadrabähu's migration. The Ganga, Kadamba, and Rästrakut dynasties were great champions of Jainism. Shravanbelgola remains the most important center for Digambars.

West India (Gujarat, Rajasthan)

A stronghold for Shvetämbar Jains. Kings like Kumärpäl and ministers like Vastupäl and Tejpäl helped build magnificent temple cities on Mount Girnär, Shatrunjay, and Äbu.

North India (Mathurä)

Mathurä was a major Jain center for almost 1000 years. The second recension of the scriptures was held here. Ujjayini was another important center, especially during Emperor Samprati's reign.
